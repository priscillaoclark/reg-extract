ACTION: Notice of proposed rulemaking and request for comments.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------

SUMMARY: FinCEN is issuing this notice of proposed rulemaking to amend=20
the relevant Bank Secrecy Act (``BSA'') information sharing rules to=20
allow certain foreign law enforcement agencies, and State and local law=20

information sharing rules is a part of the Department of the Treasury's=20
continuing effort to increase the efficiency and effectiveness of its=20
anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing policies.

SUPPLEMENTARY INFORMATION:

I. Background

A. Statutory Provisions

    On October 26, 2001, the President signed into law the Uniting and=20
Strengthening America by Providing Appropriate Tools Required to=20
Intercept and Obstruct Terrorism (``USA PATRIOT ACT'') Act of 2001,=20
Public Law 107-56 (``the Act''). Title III of the Act amends the anti-
money laundering provisions of the Bank Secrecy Act, codified at 12=20
U.S.C. 1829b and 1951-1959 and 31 U.S.C. 5311-5314 and 5316-5332, to=20
promote the prevention, detection, and prosecution of international=20
money laundering and the financing of terrorism. Regulations=20
implementing the BSA appear at 31 CFR part 103. The authority of the=20
Secretary of the Treasury (``the Secretary'') to administer the BSA has=20
been delegated to the Director of FinCEN.
    Of the Act's many goals, the facilitation of information sharing=20
among governmental entities and financial institutions for the purpose=20
of combating terrorism and money laundering is of paramount importance.=20
Section 314 of the Act furthers this goal by providing for the sharing=20
of information between the government and financial institutions, and=20
among financial institutions themselves. As with many other provisions=20
of the Act, Congress has charged the U.S. Department of the Treasury=20
with developing regulations to implement these information-sharing=20
provisions.

[[Page 58927]]

    Subsection 314(a) of the Act states in part that:

    [t]he Secretary shall * * * adopt regulations to encourage=20
further cooperation among financial institutions, their regulatory=20
authorities, and law enforcement authorities, with the specific=20
purpose of encouraging regulatory authorities and law enforcement=20
authorities to share with financial institutions information=20
regarding individuals, entities, and organizations engaged in or=20
reasonably suspected based on credible evidence of engaging in=20
terrorist acts or money laundering activities.

B. Overview of the Current Regulatory Provisions Regarding the 314(a)=20
Program

    On September 26, 2002, FinCEN published a final rule implementing=20
the authority contained in section 314(a) of the Act.\1\ That rule=20
(``the 314(a) rule'') allows FinCEN to require U.S. financial=20
institutions to search their records to determine whether they have=20
maintained an account or conducted a transaction with a person that a=20
Federal law enforcement agency has certified is suspected based on=20
credible evidence of engaging in terrorist activity or money=20
laundering.\2\ Before processing a request from a Federal law=20
enforcement agency, FinCEN also requires the requesting agency to=20
certify that, in the case of money laundering, the matter is=20
significant, and that the requesting agency has been unable to locate=20
the information sought through traditional methods of investigation and=20
analysis before attempting to use this authority (``the 314(a)=20
program'').

--

    \1\ Special Information Sharing Procedures to Deter Money=20
Laundering and Terrorist Activity, 67 FR 60579 (Sept. 26, 2002).
    \2\ 31 CFR 103.100.

    Since its inception, the 314(a) program has yielded significant=20
investigative benefits to Federal law enforcement users in terrorist=20
financing and major money laundering cases. Feedback from the=20
requesters and illustrations from sample case studies consistently=20
demonstrate how useful the program is in enhancing the scope and=20
expanding the universe of investigations. In view of the proven success=20
of the 314(a) program, FinCEN seeks to broaden access to the program as=20
outlined in the following paragraphs.

C. Objectives of Proposed Changes

a. Allowing Certain Foreign Law Enforcement Agencies To Initiate 314(a)=20
Queries
    In order to satisfy the United States' treaty obligation with=20
certain foreign governments, FinCEN is proposing to extend the use of=20
the 314(a) program to include foreign law enforcement agencies. On June=20
25, 2003, the Agreement on Mutual Legal Assistance between the United=20
States and the European Union (EU) (hereinafter, the ``U.S.-EU MLAT'')=20
was signed. Between 2004 and 2006, twenty-five bilateral implementing=20
agreements also were signed by the United States and EU Member States.=20
In 2006, the U.S.-EU MLAT, along with twenty-five bilateral=20
instruments, was submitted to the U.S. Senate for its advice and=20
consent to ratification. An additional two bilateral instruments, with=20
Romania and Bulgaria, were concluded and submitted to the Senate in=20
2007, following those countries' accession to the EU. The U.S.-EU MLAT=20
and all twenty-seven bilateral instruments were ratified by the=20
President on September 23, 2008, upon the advice and consent of the=20
U.S. Senate.
    Article 4 of the U.S.-EU MLAT (entitled ``Identification of Bank=20
Information'') obligates a requested Signatory State to search on a=20
centralized basis for bank accounts within its territory that may be=20
important to a criminal investigation in the requesting Signatory=20
State. Article 4 also contemplates that Signatory States may search for=20
information in the possession of a non-bank financial institution.=20
Under Article 4, a Signatory State receiving a request may limit the=20
scope of its obligation to provide assistance to terrorist activity and=20
money laundering offenses, and many did so in their respective=20
bilateral instruments with the United States. In addition, Article 4=20
makes clear that the United States and the EU are under an obligation=20
to ensure that the application of Article 4 does not impose=20
extraordinary burdens on States that receive search requests. Certain=20
EU States are expected to accommodate search requests from the United=20
States by querying a single centralized database which identifies all=20
bank accounts within that State. In negotiating the terms of Article 4,=20
the United States expressly envisioned that EU member States would be=20
able to access the information sharing process created by the=20
implementation of section 314(a) of the Act. Expanding that process to=20
include certain foreign law enforcement requesters would greatly=20
benefit the United States by granting law enforcement agencies in the=20
United States with reciprocal rights to obtain information about=20
matching accounts in EU member States.
    Foreign law enforcement agencies would be able to use the 314(a)=20
program in a way analogous to how Federal criminal law enforcement=20
agencies currently access the program. Thus, a foreign law enforcement=20
agency, prior to initiating a 314(a) query, would have to certify that,=20
in the case of a money laundering investigation, the matter is=20
significant, and that it has been unable to locate the information=20
sought through traditional methods of investigation and analysis before=20
attempting to use the 314(a) program. FinCEN also anticipates that the=20
foreign request will be screened initially by a Federal law enforcement=20
official serving as an attach[eacute] to the requesting jurisdiction.=20
The application of these internal procedures will help ensure that the=20
314(a) program is utilized only in significant situations, thereby=20
minimizing the cost on reporting financial institutions.
b. Allowing State and Local Law Enforcement Agencies To Initiate 314(a)=20
Queries
    By regulation, access to the 314(a) program currently is only=20
available to Federal law enforcement agencies. When the section 314(a)=20
rule was drafted, FinCEN considered expanding the process to include=20
requesters from other types of law enforcement agencies. However,=20
because of uncertainty about how the new information-sharing rules=20
would impact financial institutions, FinCEN ultimately decided to defer=20
expansion beyond Federal law enforcement agencies. FinCEN now has the=20
benefit of drawing upon six years of experience in administering the=20
section 314(a) rule. In that time, financial institutions have made=20
necessary adjustments to comply with these rules and have developed=20
more efficient ways to respond to section 314(a) requests.
    Money laundering and terror-related financial crimes are not=20
limited by jurisdiction or geography. Detection and deterrence of these=20
crimes require information sharing across all levels of investigative=20
authorities, to include State and local law enforcement, to ensure the=20
broadest United States Government defense.
    State and local law enforcement investigations run the gamut of=20
criminal violations, to include money laundering and to a lesser=20
extent, terrorist financing, and some of these investigations could=20
benefit from the use of the 314(a) program. Access to the 314(a)=20
program by State and local law enforcement would provide them a=20
platform from which they could more effectively and efficiently fill=20
information gaps, including those connected with multi-jurisdictional=20
financial transactions, in the same manner as Federal law enforcement=20
agencies. This expansion of the 314(a) program, in certain limited

[[Page 58928]]

circumstances, to include State and local law enforcement authorities,=20
would benefit overall efforts to ensure that all law enforcement=20
resources are made available to combat money laundering and terrorist=20
financing.
    Therefore, the proposal would broaden 314(a) access to allow State=20
and local law enforcement agencies to submit 314(a) queries. As is the=20
case currently with requesting Federal criminal law agencies, State and=20
local law enforcement, prior to initiating a 314(a) query, would have=20
to certify that, in the case of a money laundering investigation, the=20
matter is significant, and that it has been unable to locate the=20
information sought through traditional methods of investigation and=20
analysis before attempting to use the 314(a) program. The application=20
of these internal procedures will help ensure that the 314(a) program=20
will be utilized only in the most compelling situations, thereby=20
minimizing the cost incurred by reporting financial institutions.
c. Clarifying That FinCEN, on Its Own Behalf and on Behalf of=20
Appropriate Components of the Department of the Treasury, May Initiate=20
314(a) Queries
    FinCEN's statutory mandate includes working to identify possible=20
criminal activity to appropriate Federal, State, local, and foreign law=20
enforcement agencies, and to support ongoing criminal financial=20
investigations and prosecutions.\3\ FinCEN also routinely assists the=20
law enforcement community through proactive analyses to discover=20
trends, patterns, and common activity in the financial information=20
contained in BSA reports. FinCEN's use of the 314(a) program will=20
greatly enhance the scope and utility of its case support efforts=20
beyond the insights provided from the BSA data, thereby delivering=20
critical information about significant criminal activity on a timelier=20
basis. Accordingly, FinCEN would use the 314(a) program to submit self-
initiated 314(a) queries.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------=
--

    \3\ See 31 U.S.C. 310.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------=
--

    FinCEN assists law enforcement by providing advanced or specialized=20
analysis of BSA data on significant investigations involving offenses=20
of money laundering or terrorist financing. These investigations often=20
involve multiple locations or are otherwise linked to other=20
investigations. A single 314(a) request issued by FinCEN could more=20
efficiently coordinate and simultaneously support several=20
investigations, thereby eliminating the need for separate requests from=20
each investigating agency or jurisdiction.
    There also are instances in which FinCEN's analytical products will=20
benefit from access to the 314(a) program by providing a more complete=20
picture of financial transactions and mechanisms, as well as=20
interrelationships among investigative subjects and financial=20
transactions or entities. In addition, other appropriate components of=20
the Department of the Treasury that provide analytical support, such as=20
the Department's counter-terrorist financing and money laundering=20
efforts, will be better equipped to fulfill their missions when given=20
access to the 314(a) program. It is anticipated that the findings from=20
the use of the 314(a) program will reveal additional insights and=20
overall patterns of suspicious financial activities.

II. Section-by-Section Analysis

A. Section 103.90(a)

    FinCEN proposes to amend 31 CFR 103.90(a) by changing the=20
definition of the term ``money laundering'' to include activity that=20
would be criminalized by 18 U.S.C. 1956 or 1957 if such activity=20
occurred in the United States. The change would allow the term to be=20
applied to information requests by foreign law enforcement agencies.=20
State and local law enforcement requesters would be subject to the same=20
definition of money laundering that currently applies to Federal law=20
enforcement agencies--i.e., activity that is criminalized by 18 U.S.C.=20
1956 or 1957. Thus, in the case of a significant money laundering=20
matter, a State or local law enforcement agency seeking information=20
under the section 314(a) program would have to certify that it is=20
investigating activity that would be criminalized under 18 U.S.C. 1956=20
or 1957. Such activity could include, for example, conducting a=20
financial transaction with proceeds of murder, kidnapping, or dealing=20
in a controlled substance (as defined in section 102 of the Controlled=20
Substances Act), which is punishable as a felony under State law.\4\
-------------------------------------------------------------------------=
--

    \4\ See 18 U.S.C. 1956(c)(7) (defining the term ``specified=20
unlawful activity'' to include, inter alia, an offense listed in 18=20
U.S.C. 1961(1)).
-------------------------------------------------------------------------=
--

B. Section 103.100(a)(4)

    FinCEN proposes to add 31 CFR 103.100(a)(4), which would define a=20
``law enforcement agency'' to include a Federal, State, local, or=20
foreign law enforcement agency with criminal investigative authority,=20
provided that the foreign law enforcement agency is from a jurisdiction=20
that is a party to a treaty that provides for, or in the determination=20
of FinCEN is from a jurisdiction that otherwise allows, law enforcement=20
agencies in the United States with reciprocal access to information=20
comparable to that obtainable under section 103.100. The addition of=20
foreign law enforcement agencies would enable the United States to be=20
compliant with its obligations under the U.S.-EU MLAT, thereby=20
providing law enforcement agencies in the United States with the=20
benefit of reciprocal access to information in EU member States. The=20
U.S.-EU MLAT, and 27 bilateral instruments with EU Member States=20
implementing its terms, require each EU member State to be able to=20
search for the kind of information covered by 31 CFR 103.100 and to=20
promptly report to the requesting State the results of such a search.
    The addition of State and local law enforcement agencies would=20
provide a platform for such agencies to deal more effectively with=20
multi-jurisdictional financial transactions in the same manner as=20
Federal law enforcement agencies. Access to the 314(a) program would=20
provide State and local law enforcement agencies with another resource=20
to aide in discovering the whereabouts of stolen proceeds.

C. Section 103.100(b)(1)

    FinCEN proposes to amend section 103.100(b)(1) to make conforming=20
changes to reflect the addition of State and local law enforcement=20
agencies, and foreign law enforcement agencies, as potential requesters=20
of information. These other categories of law enforcement agencies=20
would be subject to the same standard now applicable to Federal law=20
enforcement agencies--in particular, the requirement to certify that=20
each individual, entity, or organization about which the law=20
enforcement agency is seeking information is engaged in, or is=20
reasonably suspected based on credible evidence of engaging in,=20
terrorist activity or money laundering. To further ensure that=20
financial institutions are not overwhelmed by information requests,=20
FinCEN has, since 2003, adopted an additional operating procedure that=20
requires Federal law enforcement agencies to further certify that, in=20
the case of a money laundering investigation, the matter is=20
significant. FinCEN intends to apply that same standard to State, local=20
and certain foreign law enforcement agencies.

D. Section 103.100(b)(2)

    FinCEN proposes to add new 31 CFR 103.100(b)(2) which would clarify=20
that FinCEN may request directly, on its own behalf and on behalf of=20
appropriate components of the Department of the Treasury, whether a=20
financial institution or a group of financial institutions maintains or=20
has maintained accounts

[[Page 58929]]

for, or has engaged in transactions with, specified individuals,=20
entities, or organizations. Such information requests shall be for the=20
purpose of conducting analyses to deter and detect terrorist financing=20
activity or money laundering. Adding FinCEN, itself and acting on=20
behalf of other appropriate Treasury components, as a requester of=20
information will increase the value of analytical support to law=20
enforcement.
